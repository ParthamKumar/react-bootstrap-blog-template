1.3 Project Overview
The project, titled “ArguMint: Picture the Point”, represents an interdisciplinary effort to bridge the gap between formal logic and visual cognitive processing. At its core, the project is a response to the evolving nature of digital discourse, where the sheer volume of information often necessitates a more immediate and impactful form of communication than text alone can provide. Images have historically served as a potent medium for expressing deep-seated meaning and facilitating rapid comprehension in ways that bypass the cognitive load associated with dense textual analysis. In contrast, arguments are traditionally viewed as strictly structured and logical frameworks, often perceived as dry or inaccessible to the general public. 
The genesis of this research is rooted in the Touché 2025 shared task at CLEF (Conference and Labs of the Evaluation Forum). This international campaign seeks to investigate a fundamental question in human-computer interaction: how can imagery effectively represent the essential elements of an argument to enhance its clarity, resonance, and ultimate influence. While a full academic argument typically consists of a central claim substantiated by a series of supporting premises or evidence, Our initiative focuses on a more modern, streamlined form of rhetoric. In our research, arguments are treated as succinct statements, featuring only a single, potent claim devoid of traditional supporting premises. This simplification mirrors the "soundbite" culture of modern social media and digital journalism, where a single sentence must carry the full weight of a position. 
The specific technical mandate of this project is to develop an AI system that, given such a succinct argument, can either retrieve or generate high-fidelity images that assist in conveying the central aspects of that claim. Our team has pivoted toward a generative approach, leveraging the latest advancements in diffusion models. By fine-tuning Stable Diffusion 3.5 Medium, a state-of-the-art 2-billion parameter text-to-image model, we aim to go beyond simple keyword-to-image matching. Our goal is to create a system that understands the sentiment and intent behind an argument, producing visuals that act as a "visual premise" for the textual claim. 
By participating in the Touché 2025 evaluation campaign, “ArguMint” seeks not only to provide a technical solution to image generation but also to contribute to the broader understanding of how humans perceive and validate arguments in a multimodal digital environment. The project encompasses the entire lifecycle of an AI product: from complex semantic data wrangling and high-performance GPU training on an NVIDIA RTX 4090 to the deployment of a full-stack web application designed for real-time user interaction.
1.4 Problem Statement
The fundamental problem addressed by Argu Mint: Picture the Point is the generation of contextually relevant images from textual arguments that consist of single claims without supporting premises. Unlike traditional text-to-image generation tasks where input prompts describe visual scenes explicitly, argument illustration requires the system to: 
Infer arguments such as "Celebrity culture distorts personal values" or "Urban noise pollution damages mental calm" which do not directly describe visual content. The system must infer appropriate visual representations from abstract concepts. 
According to the Touché 2025 evaluation criteria, generated images must represent the central aspects of an argument's claim. Each argument has two identified key aspects that should be visually conveyed. 
The produced image should faithfully represent the position and implications of the argument, rather than simply illustrating associated but peripheral ideas.
1.5 Technical Challenges
1.5.1 Domain Gap Between Descriptive and Argumentative Text 
Existing T2I models are predominantly trained on descriptive image captions from datasets like MS-COCO, LAION, and similar corpora. These captions describe what is visible in an image (e.g., "A dog running in a park"). Argumentative text, however, expresses opinions, claims, and value judgments that do not directly map to visual elements. This domain mismatch results in poor performance when state-of-the-art models are applied directly to argument illustration without fine-tuning. 
1.5.2 Semantic Alignment with Abstract Concepts 
Large-scale T2I models frequently struggle with attribute binding and compositional understanding [8][17]. For argument illustration, the challenge is amplified because: 
Claims often involve abstract concepts ("values," "well-being," "mental calm") 
The visual metaphors required are not explicitly stated 
Multiple valid interpretations may exist for a single argument 
1.5.3 Evaluation Complexity 
Unlike standard image generation tasks evaluated through automated metrics (FID, CLIP Score), argument illustration requires assessment of whether generated images effectively convey the argument's key aspects. The Touché challenge employs manual evaluation using NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) based on human judgment of aspect representation, making optimization and validation more complex. 
1.5.4 Quality-Alignment Trade-off 
Research has demonstrated that fine-tuning models for improved semantic alignment using human feedback can significantly reduce image quality, producing oversaturated or non-photorealistic outputs [10]. For argument illustration, both aspects are critical, images must be visually compelling while accurately representing the claim. 
1.5.5 Limited Training Data 
The CLEF argument-image dataset provides a relatively small corpus compared to the billions of image-text pairs used to train foundation models. Effective fine-tuning with limited domain-specific data while preventing overfitting and maintaining generalization is a significant challenge.
1.6 Research Objectives
The primary aim of this research is to architect and evaluate a robust, end-to-end pipeline capable of translating complex, often abstract, textual argument claims into contextually and aesthetically resonant images. To achieve this, the project is structured around several core technical and academic objectives that span the domains of Natural Language Processing (NLP), Computer Vision, and Full-Stack Engineering. 
1.6.1 Optimization of Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
A central objective of this study is to implement and optimize a fine-tuning strategy for a state-of-the-art text-to-image diffusion model—specifically Stable Diffusion 3.5 Medium. Rather than attempting a full-parameter update, which is computationally prohibitive, we utilize Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) to inject argument-specific knowledge into the model's transformer layers. The objective is to determine the ideal rank and alpha configurations that allow the model to learn the nuances of "argumentative context" without suffering from catastrophic forgetting of its base generative capabilities. 
1.6.2 Developing a Resource-Constrained Training Pipeline
Given the disparate hardware environment available, ranging from a consumer-grade RTX 4050 with 6GB VRAM to a high-end RTX 4090 with 24GB VRAM, a key research objective is the development of a memory-efficient training architecture. We seek to evaluate the efficacy of deep-learning optimization techniques, such as 8-bit quantization for large text encoders (T5-XXL), gradient checkpointing, and mixed-precision (BFloat16) training. The goal is to prove that high-fidelity generative results can be achieved on consumer-accessible hardware, effectively democratizing the use of large-scale diffusion models for niche research tasks. 
1.6.3 Enhancing Semantic Data Alignment
A significant portion of this project is dedicated to the objective of "Semantic Data Wrangling." Because standard image-caption pairs often lack argumentative depth, we aim to develop a preprocessing pipeline that semantically matches images to arguments using GTE-Large sentence transformers. By generating 1024-dimensional embeddings and applying cosine similarity thresholds (specifically a threshold of 0.25), we objective to create a "Golden Dataset" where every training pair is mathematically and contextually relevant to the 128 arguments provided by the CLEF Touché task.
1.7 Motivation and Challenges
The development of ArguMint was not merely a linear execution of a pre-defined plan but rather an iterative journey marked by significant technical pivots, logistical hurdles, and profound "breakthrough" moments. While the project successfully met the requirements of the Touché 2025 task, the motivation behind our specific implementation choices was deeply rooted in overcoming real-world constraints that are often glossed over in purely theoretical research.
1.7.1 The Computational VRAM Limit and the Strategic Pivot
One of the primary motivations for our research was to explore the limits of modern Diffusion Transformers (DiT) under strict hardware constraints. Initially, our team held the high-level ambition of employing Stable Diffusion 3.5 Large to ensure the highest possible generative fidelity. However, the early stages of development presented a stark reality: the "Large" model was computationally prohibitive for our available hardware. Balancing development on an NVIDIA RTX 4050 (6GB VRAM) and heavy training on an RTX 4090 (24GB VRAM), we realized that the “Large” variant exceeded our VRAM overhead for a stable, high-resolution training pipeline. 
This led to a critical, surgical pivot toward Stable Diffusion 3.5 Medium. This 2-billion parameter model offered an ideal equilibrium, providing enough complexity to understand argumentative nuance while remaining within the 24GB memory limit required for efficient LoRA fine-tuning. This challenge forced us to prioritize optimization over raw scale, leading to the implementation of 8-bit quantization for the T5-XXL text encoder and gradient checkpointing—techniques that ultimately defined the technical rigor of our project. 
1.7.2 The "Data Wrangling" Ordeal
Another significant motivation was the desire to move beyond generic datasets and tackle the specific problem of "Argument-to-Image" semantic alignment. The official CLEF dataset provided a rich collection of images, but we quickly discovered that a model is only as robust as the data it consumes. The raw data was often "noisy," containing images that were semantically detached from the core arguments or technically unsuitable (e.g., corrupt files or irrelevant metadata). 
We undertook an extensive data-clearing and preprocessing ordeal, which involved extracting 128 distinctive arguments and matching them to over 14,000 images using GTE-Large semantic embeddings. Setting a strict cosine similarity threshold of 0.25 was a calculated risk; it ensured that the model learned only high-quality visual-textual mappings, effectively "teaching" the AI the difference between a generic picture and a visual argument. Spot-checking these thousands of matches for quality was a labor-intensive but necessary component of our methodology. 
1.7.3 The Breakthrough: From Pixels to Persuasion
The most powerful motivation during the grueling 300-hour training cycle was the anticipation of the first high-resolution outputs. Fine-tuning is often a "black box" process where the researcher waits for convergence, and for ArguMint, the "aha!" moment came during our transition from 256×256 to 512×512 resolution. 
Seeing the model successfully translate an abstract claim, such as the environmental impact of forest destruction, into a contextually resonant image was a validation of our entire research approach. While the model still faces challenges with certain complex details like facial features, the realization that it could successfully learn to generate images that serve as a "visual premise" for a textual claim was the definitive success of the FYP-I phase. This shift from technical struggle to successful visualization remains the core inspiration for the ongoing refinements in our FYP-II cycle.

Related Work
2.1 Background and Justification 
2.1.1 Evolution of Text-to-Image Generation 
The field of text-to-image generation has witnessed remarkable progress over the past decade, transitioning from domain-specific generative adversarial networks (GANs) to general-purpose transformer and diffusion-based models. Early approaches such as StackGAN and AttnGAN demonstrated the feasibility of generating images from text but were constrained to narrow domains and suffered from artifacts and limited resolution [1][2]. 
The introduction of DALL-E by OpenAI in 2021 marked a paradigm shift, demonstrating that scaling transformer architectures to 12 billion parameters and training on 250 million image-text pairs could achieve impressive zero-shot generation capabilities [3]. This was followed by significant advances in latent diffusion models (LDMs), which reduced computational requirements by operating in compressed latent spaces rather than pixel space [4]. 
Current state-of-the-art models including Stable Diffusion 3.5, PixArt-Σ, and HiDream have pushed the boundaries of resolution, prompt adherence, and generation efficiency [5][6][7]. However, these models are primarily trained on descriptive image captions rather than abstract argumentative text, creating a significant domain gap for argument illustration tasks. 
2.1.2 The Argument Illustration Challenge 
The Touché 2025 shared task on "Image Retrieval/Generation for Arguments" at CLEF (Conference and Labs of the Evaluation Forum) presents a novel challenge: given an argument consisting of a single claim without supporting premises, systems must find or generate images that help convey central aspects of the argument's claim. This task 
bridges computational argumentation and visual communication, areas that have traditionally been studied in isolation. 
In contrast to traditional text-to-image generation, which typically involves prompts that depict specific visual scenarios (for instance, "a sunset over mountains"), argument illustration necessitates the interpretation of abstract assertions such as "Excessive homework diminishes student well-being" or "Energy drinks interfere with natural sleep patterns" and their conversion into significant visual depictions. The difficulty resides in encapsulating the core of an argument through the imagery produced. 
2.1.3 Project Justification 
Argu Mint: Picture the Point addresses this research gap by developing a specialized text-to-image generation system fine-tuned for argument illustration. Our approach builds upon Stable Diffusion 3.5 Medium, leveraging its advanced architecture while adapting it for the unique requirements of argumentative text: 
Training on 14,000 argument-image pairs from the CLEF dataset to bridge the gap between argumentative language and visual representation. 
Utilizing the SD3 triple text encoder architecture (dual CLIP and T5 encoders) for comprehensive interpretation of abstract claims. 
Employing classifier-free guidance and flow matching scheduling to generate images that capture key aspects identified in the evaluation criteria. 
The practical applications of this research extend to educational technology (visualizing debate topics), journalism (illustrating opinion pieces), legal documentation (representing case arguments), and persuasive communication design.
2.2 Foundational Autoregressive Approaches 
This section provides a comprehensive review of the literature on text-to-image generation, organized thematically to trace the evolution of architectures, methodologies, and capabilities. 
2.2.1 Zero-Shot Text-to-Image Generation (DALL-E) 
Ramesh et al. [3] introduced DALL-E, a groundbreaking two-stage approach for zero-shot text-to-image generation. The architecture employs a discrete variational autoencoder (dVAE) to compress 256×256 RGB images into 32×32 grids of image tokens, followed by a 12-billion parameter autoregressive transformer that models the joint distribution of text and image tokens. Trained on 250 million image-text pairs collected from the internet, DALL-E demonstrated remarkable generalization capabilities, generating high-quality images on datasets like MS-COCO without task-specific training. 
The model's key innovation lies in treating text-to-image generation as a sequence modeling problem, enabling the application of transformer architectures that had proven successful in natural language processing. The reranking strategy, which selects the best image from 32 generated samples using CLIP, further improved output quality. However, the initial tokenization process can lose high-frequency details, and the model exhibits inconsistent performance on compositional tasks such as combining novel concepts (e.g., "a hedgehog wearing a Christmas sweater"). 
2.2.2 CogView: Transformer-Based Generation 
Ding et al. [12] presented CogView, a 4-billion parameter transformer model utilizing a Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQ-VAE) tokenizer. The model demonstrated scalability and flexibility in fine-tuning for various downstream tasks. However, the autoregressive generation process results in slower inference compared to diffusion-based alternatives, and the initial 256×256 resolution images are compressed to 32×32 tokens, potentially causing blurriness. The training on 30 million Chinese text-image pairs also introduced language and cultural biases in the generated content. 
2.2.3 Parti: Pathways Autoregressive Text-to-Image 
Yu et al. [11] developed Parti, which conceptualizes image generation as a translation task from text tokens to image tokens. The model excels at content-rich, complex image generation with strong prompt alignment and features a modular architecture facilitating component-wise improvements. However, the autoregressive bottleneck results in slow generation, and the model requires substantial computational resources (TPUv4 clusters), limiting accessibility for research and practical applications. 
2.3 Diffusion-Based Architectures 
2.3.1 Latent Diffusion Models (Stable Diffusion) 
Rombach et al. [4] introduced Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs), representing a significant advance in computational efficiency for diffusion-based generation. The two-stage approach first trains a VAE to compress images into a lower-dimensional latent space, then applies the diffusion process in this efficient representation. A cross-attention mechanism enables flexible conditioning on text prompts, bounding boxes, or other modalities. 
LDMs achieved state-of-the-art results on image inpainting and class-conditional synthesis while dramatically reducing training and inference costs. The approach allows the computationally expensive autoencoder to be trained once and reused across multiple diffusion model configurations. However, the compression into latent space can result in loss of fine-grained details, and performance is sensitive to the downsampling factor employed. 
2.3.2 GLIDE: Text-Guided Diffusion Models 
Nichol et al. [13] presented GLIDE, comparing CLIP guidance and classifier-free guidance for text-conditioned diffusion. The study found that classifier-free guidance produces more photorealistic and caption-aligned images. Despite being smaller than DALL-E, GLIDE achieved superior image quality and demonstrated strong generalization to complex prompts. The primary limitation is slower inference compared to GAN-based approaches, and the lack of publicly available code has limited community adoption and fine-tuning research. 
2.3.3 Hierarchical Text-Conditional Generation (unCLIP/DALL-E 2) 
Ramesh et al. [8] introduced unCLIP, a two-stage model that first generates CLIP image embeddings from text using an autoregressive prior, then decodes these embeddings into images using a diffusion decoder. The approach achieves improved image diversity compared to systems like GLIDE, with an impressive FID score of 10.39. The CLIP embedding space enables zero-shot semantic modification of images based on text descriptions. 
However, the model struggles with attribute binding—associating specific attributes with distinct objects—and cannot reliably generate correctly spelled text within images. The decoder's reliance on 64×64 base resolution limits detail in complex scenes, suggesting avenues for future improvement. 
2.4 Diffusion Transformers and Recent Advances 
2.4.1 PixArt-Σ: Weak-to-Strong Training 
Chen et al. [5] developed PixArt-Σ, a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) capable of directly generating 4K resolution images. The model employs a weak-to-strong training strategy, leveraging pretrained PixArt-α weights for efficient training on higher-quality data. An efficient token compression framework enables high-resolution synthesis while maintaining a relatively small parameter count of 0.6 billion—considerably smaller than models like SDXL. 
PixArt-Σ excels at following complex, long text prompts through improved captioning using Share Captioner 
instead of LLAVA. Despite these advances, the model still struggles with text generation within images and hand/face rendering. Future research directions include enhanced prompt handling, improved face generation, and security considerations for bias mitigation. 
2.4.2 HiDream-I1: Sparse Diffusion Transformer 
Cai et al. [7] introduced HiDream-I1-Fast, a lightweight variant of a 17-billion parameter sparse diffusion transformer utilizing Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) for efficient generation. The model is optimized for speed and reduced hardware requirements, enabling high-quality synthesis on single-GPU setups while supporting instruction-based image editing through HiDream-E1. 
The open-source availability of weights and code facilitates adaptation and research. However, the fast variant exhibits slightly lower fidelity compared to full versions, and integration of editing pipelines requires careful setup. The architecture presents opportunities for fine-tuning on specialized tasks such as argument-to-image mapping. 
2.4.3 MMaDA: Multimodal Diffusion Language Models 
Yang et al. [14] presented MMaDA, a unified 8-billion parameter diffusion architecture designed for text-to-image generation, multimodal understanding, and textual reasoning. The model employs a modality-agnostic diffusion framework with chain-of-thought fine-tuning and a unified reinforcement learning objective (UniGRPO). 
MMaDA outperforms strong baselines including SDXL and Janus on text-to-image benchmarks while maintaining open-source accessibility. The unified architecture may require careful tuning for purely image-focused tasks, but presents potential for adaptation to argument illustration applications requiring integrated reasoning and generation capabilities. 
2.5 Controllability and Grounding Approaches 
2.5.1 Visual Programming for Generation and Evaluation 
Cho et al. [15] introduced VPGEN and VPEVAL, frameworks utilizing visual programming for T2I generation and evaluation. VPGEN implements a three-step process: an LLM generates objects and counts, creates layouts with bounding boxes, and guides a T2I model for final image generation. VPEVAL employs an LLM to generate evaluation programs calling specialized visual modules for scoring and explanation. 
These frameworks provide superior control over object counts, spatial relationships, and scales while offering enhanced interpretability through step-by-step generation. The LLM-based approach handles unseen objects beyond training distributions. However, the multi-step pipeline introduces complexity and potential failure points, and output quality depends on underlying layout-to-image model performance. 
2.5.2 ObjectDiffusion: Grounded Generation 
Süleyman and Biricik [9] developed ObjectDiffusion, grounding T2I diffusion models through explicit layout inputs. Users control object placement and appearance via bounding boxes and object names alongside text prompts. The model achieves high-precision control over spatial relationships and supports open-vocabulary generation, outperforming existing models on controllable generation metrics. 
The approach addresses critical controllability requirements for practical applications but requires additional user inputs, increasing workflow complexity compared to zero-shot models. The two-stage architecture adds computational overhead but enables the precise control necessary for structured image synthesis. 
2.6 Personalization and Alignment 
2.6.1 Imagine Yourself: Tuning-Free Personalization 
The Meta GenAI team [16] introduced Imagine Yourself, a tuning-free personalization framework for diffusion-based generation. The approach achieves better prompt alignment for complex instructions without requiring model fine-tuning for individual subjects. However, performance on complex poses and unusual prompt combinations remains imperfect. The framework primarily addresses human subjects, with extension to non-human subjects (pets, art styles, objects, fictional characters) remaining underexplored. 
2.7 Aligning Models with Human Feedback 
Lee and Liu [10] developed a three-stage fine-tuning method using human feedback to enhance text-to-image alignment. Building on the success of RLHF techniques in language models like GPT-3, the approach achieved up to 47% improvement in image-text alignment, generating objects with specified colors, counts, and backgrounds more accurately than pretrained models. 
A critical finding is that naive fine-tuning with human feedback can significantly reduce image quality, producing oversaturated or non-photorealistic outputs with reduced diversity. This quality-alignment trade-off represents a fundamental challenge for practical deployment of aligned T2I models. 
2.8 Semantic Enhancement with Large Language Models 
2.8.1 LLM4GEN: Leveraging LLM Representations 
Liu et al. [17] proposed LLM4GEN, a plug-and-play component improving T2I generation from complex instructions. The Cross-Adapter Module (CAM) combines LLM text understanding with standard T2I text encoders, while entity-guided regularization loss ensures correct attribute-object binding. 
The approach addresses the limited capacity of CLIP-based text encoders for intricate semantic details and achieves strong performance across benchmarks including FID scores. Unlike previous methods requiring separate LLMs or vast training data, LLM4GEN uses only 10 million data points for efficient training. The introduction of DensePrompts addresses the lack of comprehensive benchmarks for long, detailed descriptions. 
2.9 Unified Multimodal Architectures 
2.9.1 Chameleon: Mixed-Modal Foundation Models 
The Chameleon Team [18] developed a family of early-fusion, token-based mixed models processing arbitrary sequences of text, images, and code. The fully token-based representation enables a unified transformer architecture where images and text are converted to tokens and processed together. 
The approach demonstrates strong performance across tasks when trained on large interleaved datasets with stability enhancements. However, optimization challenges arise from different distributions of text and image tokens, causing training instability. Image tokenization (1024 tokens from an 8192-size codebook) creates scalability concerns with increased input lengths affecting training and inference speed.

Implementation
5.1 Dataset
5.1.1 Dataset Description
The research framework is built upon the Image Retrieval for Arguments dataset, which was curated for the Touché 2025 shared task [28]. Within this corpus, 128 argumentative claims are explored across 27 distinct sociopolitical and cultural topics. The dataset comprises approximately 32,339 web-crawled images, providing a vast multimodal landscape for the model to navigate. Each entry is stored in a hierarchical directory structure that preserves the original web context, ensuring that the visual data remains anchored to its source.

At the root level, the core of the dataset is defined by the arguments.xml file. This repository contains the ground-truth data, including unique identification codes, broader topics, and the specific claims being made. The visual assets are organized within a nested images/ directory, where each image ID is mapped to a folder containing metadata such as raw captions, OCR-extracted text, and perceptual hashes. Furthermore, the dataset includes comprehensive pages/ subdirectories. These store the original webpage snapshots, search rankings, and XPath references, which are critical for understanding the visual-rhetorical context of the image. This comprehensive structure, detailed on the Zenodo repository, allows the preprocessing pipeline to fuse local image features with global web evidence.

The foundational model was fine-tuned using parameter-efficient techniques, specifically Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [22]. This approach allowed for the modification of the underlying transformer layers without the prohibitive computational cost of full-parameter fine-tuning. Memory constraints were further addressed through the use of 8-bit quantization for optimization routines [23], which maintained numerical stability while significantly reducing the hardware footprint. During this stage, the training pipeline [24] prioritized the alignment of images with the primary argumentative claims, establishing a functional mapping between logical premises and visual motifs.

5.2.2 Phase 2: Semantic Refinement and Optimization
The second stage of implementation introduced rigorous optimizations to enhance the relevance and aesthetic quality of the synthesized outputs. Central to this phase was the integration of a multi-stage contrastive learning framework, which utilized high-capacity text embeddings [20] to refine the model's understanding of subtle rhetorical nuances. The training architecture was upgraded to incorporate rectified flow transformers [21], which linearize the mapping between Gaussian noise and the data distribution for more efficient sampling.

Figure 5: Phase 2 Loss Curve
Technical enhancements in this refined phase [27] included the adoption of a minimum signal-to-noise ratio (Min-SNR) weighting strategy [25]. By adjusting the loss contribution based on the noise level, the training process became more robust against high-frequency artifacts. Additionally, the text encoder itself was subjected to specialized adaptation, ensuring that the semantic representations provided to the generative backbone were closely aligned with the "argument-focused" captions. The training logic also explored sigmoid loss functions [26] to stabilize the language-image pre-training. These optimizations resulted in a model that does not merely describe an image but synthesizes visual content that serves as a rhetorical extension of a logical claim.

5.3 Building the Application
The final component of the project involves the development of a centralized platform to facilitate the testing and generation of argument-aligned imagery. This application is constructed using a modern full-stack architecture, featuring a reactive frontend for real-time user interaction and a scalable backend capable of handling model inference tasks. The primary objective of this interface is to provide a sandbox environment where users can input argumentative claims and observe the system’s ability to generate supportive visual evidence.

While the current implementation serves as a testing ground for the Touché task, the architecture is designed with modularity in mind. This ensures that the platform can be adapted for future domain-specific use cases, most notably in the field of e-learning. In such a scenario, the system could automatically generate supporting visual aids for complex logical concepts, thereby enhancing the instructional value of digital educational content.

Conclusion


ArguMint: Picture the Point successfully illustrates the potential of the fusion of modern diffusion-based generative models with semantic understanding techniques to tackle the complex challenge of illustration of arguments. The project examined the possibility of conveying abstract arguments in a visual way that can help users grasp the main point and emotional content of an argument. The research is part of the Touché 2025 shared task, an initiative that sets the groundwork for the convergence of computational argumentation, multimodal AI, and visual, human-centered communication. 

The system's implementation process covered the entire lifecycle of an AI-based application, from preprocessing large-scale datasets to semantic alignment, fine-tuning the model, deployment, and evaluation. The project was carried out using the Stable Diffusion 3.5 Medium and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) models, which provided a balance between performance and computational efficiency. The project used the Stable Diffusion 3.5 Medium and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) models, offering an efficient balance between model performance and computational resources. The 8-bit quantization, gradient checkpointing, mixed-precision training and Min-SNR [25]  weighting techniques allowed the model to perform well under hardware constraints without compromising visual quality and semantic consistency. 

The key step of this research was creating a high-quality semantic preprocessing pipeline to match argumentative claims with relevant visual data. The project resulted in a curated “Golden Dataset”, using GTE-Large embeddings, OCR extraction, Web-context analysis and cosine similarity matching, which greatly enhanced the relevance of outputs. The model's ability to produce images as visual extensions of rhetorical claims was further improved by the transition from Phase I baseline training of the model to Phase II semantic refinement to enhance contextual understanding of the model. 






Beyond the generative model, the project also resulted in a usable full-stack application that facilitates user authentication, image generation, the management of galleries, real-time tracking of the generation of assets, and persistent inference tracking. The platform was found to be reliable through functional and security testing, and it is designed to be modular to allow for easy scalability in the future for other improvements and domain-specific applications. 

In general, ArguMint lays a solid groundwork for future exploration of argument-aware image generation and multimodal reasoning systems. While there are still avenues for improvement, including the introduction of more complex facial structures, the use of abstract symbols, and final CLEF human evaluation scores, the project demonstrates the potential for creating semantically similar visual arguments with current diffusion architectures. Possible future work includes scaling the dataset to more dimensions, incorporating reinforcement learning with human feedback, optimizing mechanism to make the system more controllable, and scaling the system to new domains, such as education, journalism, and persuasive communication, where visual argumentation can greatly help the user's understanding and engagement. 




















Future Works

Argument-Aware Image Editing and Iterative Refinement 
One of the primary improvements of ArguMint in the future will be the ability to edit images rather than just creating a single image. This would enable users to further manipulate the output of the visuals to the degree they would like, such as adding or taking away emotional intensity, being more specific about what components they want to highlight, or make things simpler in composition, but keep the original meaning and argument. Combining diffusion-based image editing with the already established LoRA adapted Stable Diffusion pipeline could enable such iterative refinement, providing more accurate, flexible, and user-driven visuals to represent complex arguments.

Multi-Image Argument Narratives for Visual Storytelling
Another promising avenue of research is the possibility of scaling up the system from outputs of a single image to outputs of structured multi-image narratives. In this arrangement, one argumentative claim would be broken down into a series of argumentative visuals that each supported particular parts of the claim: cause, effect, evidence, and conclusion. This would now become visual storytelling and make it easier to convey complex or multi-layer arguments. In order to achieve a coherent sequence and reinforce the rhetorical effectiveness, consistency of the sematics and style inside the generated sequence would be paramount.

Human-in-the-Loop Refinement and Feedback-Driven Alignment
Further studies could also include the use of human feedback in the generation pipeline to enhance alignment and controllability. The inclusion of a human-in-the-loop mechanism would allow users or evaluators to give feedback on the quality of the images generated in terms of how well they support the intended argument, which could then be used to fine-tune or guide the model. The reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) or preference-based optimization methods would assist the system to acquire more accurate visual understanding of abstract statements. This would considerably improve the reliability in the real world applications like education, journalism and persuasive communications.
